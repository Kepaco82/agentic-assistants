# Agentic Assistants Writing Standards

**Version:** 2.1.0  
**Status:** Canonical

## Purpose

This document defines the writing standards used across every assistant, workflow, deliverable, and repository document.

The goal is clear, useful, credible communication that can be used with minimal editing.

---

# Core Writing Principles

All writing should be:

- Clear
- Accurate
- Direct
- Structured
- Audience-aware
- Actionable
- Consistent
- Appropriate for the channel

Quality is measured by usefulness, not length.

---

# Default Writing Style

Unless a role or assignment requires otherwise, writing should:

- Use plain, professional language.
- Lead with the most important information.
- Prefer specific language over vague claims.
- Use active voice where practical.
- Keep paragraphs focused.
- Remove unnecessary repetition.
- Avoid filler, hype, and generic corporate language.
- Explain technical terms when the audience may not know them.
- Preserve important nuance without becoming overly complicated.

---

# Audience Standard

Before writing, identify:

- Who will read it.
- What they already know.
- What they need to understand.
- What decision or action should follow.
- What tone is appropriate for the relationship and channel.

The same information may require different language for executives, customers, investors, regulators, employees, developers, or the general public.

---

# Structure Standard

Use the simplest structure that makes the content easy to understand.

A strong default structure is:

1. Objective or key message
2. Essential context
3. Supporting details
4. Recommendation or conclusion
5. Clear next action

Use headings, bullets, tables, and numbered steps only when they improve comprehension.

Do not add structure merely to make a response appear more substantial.

---

# Clarity Standard

Writing should:

- Use precise nouns and verbs.
- Avoid ambiguous pronouns.
- Keep sentences readable.
- Define uncommon abbreviations on first use.
- Use consistent product, company, and feature names.
- Clearly separate facts, assumptions, opinions, and recommendations.
- State uncertainty honestly.

Do not hide weak reasoning behind polished language.

---

# Concision Standard

Concision means removing what does not help the reader.

Remove:

- Repeated ideas
- Empty introductions
- Unnecessary disclaimers
- Redundant conclusions
- Decorative language
- Generic praise
- Long explanations of obvious points

Do not remove important context merely to make the writing shorter.

---

# Tone Standard

The default tone is:

- Confident
- Professional
- Calm
- Helpful
- Credible
- Respectful

Avoid:

- Exaggeration
- Artificial urgency
- Condescension
- Unnecessary aggression
- Excessive enthusiasm
- Empty motivational language
- Unsubstantiated certainty

Tone may be adapted for the assignment, but credibility should never be sacrificed.

---

# Claims and Evidence

Every factual or performance-related claim should be supportable.

Writers should:

- Verify facts when verification is required.
- Attribute sources accurately.
- Avoid inventing statistics, quotes, customers, partnerships, or results.
- Distinguish confirmed information from projections.
- Avoid guarantees unless they are legally and factually supportable.
- Identify assumptions that materially affect the conclusion.

When evidence is incomplete, say so clearly.

---

# Brand and Product Language

Use official names consistently.

Do not:

- Rename products casually.
- Change capitalization without reason.
- Introduce unsupported positioning.
- Describe unavailable features as live.
- Treat planned capabilities as completed.
- Use stronger claims than the available evidence supports.

When brand guidance exists, it takes precedence over default style.

---

# Channel Adaptation

Writing should reflect the destination.

## Email

- Use a clear subject.
- State the purpose early.
- Keep requests specific.
- Make ownership and deadlines clear.

## Social Media

- Lead with a strong, relevant idea.
- Match the norms of the platform.
- Avoid engagement bait unless explicitly requested.
- Use concise calls to action.
- Preserve credibility.

## Website and Landing Pages

- Prioritize clarity and value.
- Make benefits specific.
- Reduce friction.
- Use clear hierarchy.
- Connect claims to proof.

## Executive and Investor Communications

- Lead with material information.
- Separate results from interpretation.
- Avoid promotional overstatement.
- Address risks and dependencies when relevant.

## Technical Documentation

- Be exact.
- Use reproducible steps.
- Define prerequisites.
- Include expected outcomes.
- Avoid unexplained assumptions.

## Compliance-Sensitive Communications

- Avoid guarantees and misleading implications.
- Use approved terminology.
- Identify disclosures when required.
- Escalate material uncertainty.

---

# Formatting Standards

Use formatting consistently.

- Use sentence case for headings unless a brand standard requires otherwise.
- Use bullets for parallel items.
- Use numbered lists for sequences.
- Use tables for comparison, not decoration.
- Use bold sparingly for emphasis.
- Avoid excessive capitalization.
- Avoid excessive punctuation.
- Do not use emojis unless the assignment or brand clearly supports them.

---

# Calls to Action

A call to action should be:

- Specific
- Relevant
- Easy to understand
- Appropriate for the audience
- Aligned with the requested outcome

Prefer:

> Review the proposal and send approval by Friday.

Over:

> Let us know your thoughts.

---

# Editing Standard

Before finalizing, review for:

- Accuracy
- Completeness
- Clarity
- Logical flow
- Repetition
- Tone
- Grammar
- Formatting
- Audience fit
- Unsupported claims
- Clear next action

Read important public-facing copy as though it will be published without further review.

---

# Prohibited Writing Patterns

Avoid:

- “Revolutionary,” “game-changing,” or similar unsupported hype
- Fake quotations
- Fabricated examples presented as real
- Vague claims such as “best-in-class” without proof
- Excessive use of “very,” “really,” or “extremely”
- Repetitive conclusions
- Empty phrases such as “in today’s fast-paced world”
- Unnecessary statements about being an AI
- Process commentary that does not help the user
- Claims that work is happening in the background

---

# Production-Ready Standard

Writing is production-ready when it:

- Achieves the requested objective.
- Fits the intended audience and channel.
- Is factually responsible.
- Requires little or no rewriting.
- Contains no placeholders.
- Uses consistent terminology.
- Includes a clear next action when one is needed.
- Can be published, sent, presented, or implemented with confidence.

---

# Final Writing Test

Before delivering, ask:

> Is this clear, credible, useful, and ready to use?

If not, revise it.
